# Seasonal Gardening Guide

## Spring Planning and Planting

Begin garden planning 6-8 weeks before the last expected frost date. Start warm-season crops indoors under lights: tomatoes, peppers, and eggplant need 6-8 weeks of indoor growing before transplanting. Direct-sow cold-hardy crops as soon as soil can be worked: peas, lettuce, spinach, radishes, and kale tolerate frost and can be planted 4-6 weeks before the last frost date. Harden off indoor seedlings by gradually exposing them to outdoor conditions over 7-10 days before transplanting. Work compost into garden beds (2-4 inches) before planting. Test soil and amend based on results — most vegetables prefer pH 6.0-7.0.

## Summer Maintenance

Water deeply and less frequently rather than shallow daily watering — this encourages deep root growth. Most vegetables need 1-1.5 inches of water per week. Water in the morning to reduce evaporation and disease. Mulch 2-4 inches deep around plants to retain moisture, suppress weeds, and regulate soil temperature. Side-dress heavy feeders (tomatoes, corn, squash) with compost or balanced fertilizer every 3-4 weeks. Scout for pests daily — hand-pick large insects, spray aphids with a strong water jet, use row covers to prevent moths from laying eggs on brassicas. Harvest regularly to encourage continued production — a single overripe zucchini signals the plant to stop producing.

## Fall Harvest and Extension

Plant fall crops in mid to late summer: broccoli, cabbage, Brussels sprouts, carrots, beets, turnips, and lettuces. Many crops improve in flavor after light frost — kale, carrots, parsnips, and Brussels sprouts convert starch to sugar as a natural antifreeze response. Extend the season with row covers (floating fabric that adds 4-8°F of frost protection), cold frames (bottomless boxes with transparent lids), or low tunnels (hoops covered with plastic). Harvest winter squash after the first light frost but before a hard freeze — cure in a warm dry area for 10 days before storing. Save seeds from your best plants for next year (open-pollinated varieties only).

## Winter Preparation

After the final harvest, plant cover crops (winter rye, crimson clover, hairy vetch) to protect and improve soil over winter. Cover crops prevent erosion, suppress weeds, fix nitrogen (legumes), and add organic matter when turned under in spring. Alternatively, spread 4-6 inches of leaves or straw over beds as a winter mulch. Clean and sharpen tools before storage — oil metal surfaces to prevent rust. Drain and store hoses before freezing temperatures. Review the season's successes and failures — keep a garden journal noting varieties, planting dates, yields, and pest issues. Order seeds in winter while selection is best.

## Perennial Food Systems

Establish perennial food plants that produce year after year without replanting. Fruit trees (apple, pear, cherry, plum) take 3-5 years to produce but then yield for decades. Berry bushes (blueberry, raspberry, blackberry, elderberry) establish in 1-2 years. Asparagus produces for 15-20 years after a 2-year establishment period. Rhubarb, horseradish, Jerusalem artichoke, and many herbs (rosemary, thyme, oregano, chives) are perennial and require minimal maintenance. Plant perennials in dedicated beds where they will not be disturbed by annual garden tilling. Mulch heavily and prune as needed.
