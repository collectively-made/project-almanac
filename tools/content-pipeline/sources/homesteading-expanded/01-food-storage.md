# Long-Term Food Storage

## Shelf-Stable Pantry Basics

A well-stocked pantry forms the foundation of food security. These staples store for 1-5+ years when kept cool, dry, and sealed: white rice (indefinite if sealed with oxygen absorbers), dried beans and lentils (5+ years), oats (2-3 years), pasta (2-3 years), salt (indefinite), sugar (indefinite), honey (indefinite — crystallization is normal, gently warm to re-liquefy), powdered milk (2-3 years), cooking oil (1-2 years), vinegar (indefinite), baking soda (indefinite), dried herbs and spices (1-3 years), canned goods (2-5 years past the date, as long as the can is not bulging, rusted through, or deeply dented).

## Mylar Bag Storage

For truly long-term storage (10-25+ years), use Mylar bags with oxygen absorbers inside 5-gallon food-grade buckets. Fill Mylar bags with dry goods (moisture content must be below 10% — do not use this method for brown rice, nuts, or high-fat foods). Place 300cc oxygen absorbers per gallon of volume (2000cc for a 5-gallon bag). Seal the Mylar bag with a hot iron or hair straightener — the seal must be airtight. Place the sealed Mylar bag inside the bucket and seal the bucket lid. Label with contents and date. Store in a cool, dark location — every 10°F decrease in temperature roughly doubles shelf life.

## Rotation Systems

Use the FIFO method: First In, First Out. When adding new supplies, place them behind existing stock. Mark purchase dates clearly on all items. Can rotation shelves (commercially available or DIY from angled shelving) automatically move the oldest cans to the front. Build your stored food supply from items your family actually eats — this ensures rotation happens naturally through regular use. Aim to rotate through your entire pantry within 1-2 years. Discard any canned goods that are bulging, leaking, or have off odors when opened.

## Grain Storage and Milling

Whole grain berries (wheat, rye, spelt) store for 25+ years in sealed Mylar with oxygen absorbers — far longer than flour, which goes rancid within 1-2 years. A hand-cranked grain mill converts stored grain into fresh flour for bread, pasta, and baking. Manual grain mills range from simple hand-crank models ($50-100) to high-quality burr mills ($200-400) that can also crack corn and process other grains. Freshly milled flour has more nutrients and better flavor than commercial flour. Store at least 25-30 pounds of grain per person per month if relying on it as a primary calorie source.

## Garden Seed Storage

Beyond food storage, store seeds for future growing seasons. Open-pollinated (non-hybrid) varieties can be saved and replanted year after year. Store seeds in airtight containers (mason jars with desiccant packets) in a cool, dark location — a refrigerator is ideal. Most vegetable seeds remain viable for 2-5 years: tomato (4-5 years), pepper (2-3 years), squash (4-5 years), beans (3-4 years), lettuce (1-3 years), corn (1-2 years). Test germination before depending on old seeds: place 10 seeds on a damp paper towel in a plastic bag, keep warm for 7-10 days, count how many sprout. Below 50% germination, plant extra or acquire fresh seed.
