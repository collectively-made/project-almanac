# Livestock Management

## Fencing Fundamentals

Good fencing is the foundation of livestock management. For cattle, use high-tensile wire or barbed wire (4-5 strands, posts every 12-15 feet). For goats, woven wire or cattle panels at least 4 feet high — goats will find any gap. For chickens, use hardware cloth (1/2 inch mesh) buried 12 inches underground to prevent predator digging. Electric fencing is effective and economical for most livestock: a solar-powered charger with polytape or polywire provides a flexible, movable fence system ideal for rotational grazing. Test your electric fence regularly — vegetation growing on the wire grounds out the charge.

## Rotational Grazing

Divide your pasture into paddocks and rotate animals every 1-4 weeks depending on grass growth. After animals leave a paddock, the grass needs 21-45 days of rest to regrow (longer in dry conditions, shorter in the growing season). Benefits of rotation: prevents overgrazing, breaks parasite cycles, builds soil fertility, and increases pasture productivity by up to 70% compared to continuous grazing. Follow cattle with chickens — they scratch through manure, eat fly larvae, and spread nutrients evenly. Start with at least 4 paddocks to maintain a proper rotation cycle.

## Winter Livestock Care

In cold climates, livestock need dry shelter more than warmth — a three-sided shed open to the south provides wind protection while allowing ventilation (moisture buildup causes more health problems than cold). Increase feed by 10-20% in winter — animals burn more calories maintaining body temperature. Ensure water doesn't freeze: heated water buckets, insulated troughs, or twice-daily ice breaking. Chickens reduce egg production in winter due to decreased daylight — supplemental light (a timer-controlled bulb providing 14-16 hours of total light) maintains production. Hay consumption for goats: approximately 4-5 pounds per day per adult goat in winter.

## Basic Animal Health

Learn to recognize signs of illness: lethargy, loss of appetite, isolating from the herd, abnormal droppings, discharge from eyes or nose, limping, or rapid breathing. Maintain a basic livestock first aid kit: wound spray (Blu-Kote or equivalent), electrolyte powder, thermometer (normal temp varies: cattle 101.5°F, goats 102-103°F, chickens 105-107°F), syringes for administering medication, hoof trimmers, and clean bandaging material. Establish a relationship with a large-animal veterinarian before you need one in an emergency. Quarantine any new animals for 2-3 weeks before introducing them to your existing herd.

## Breeding and Reproduction

For a self-sustaining homestead, plan breeding cycles carefully. Chickens: a rooster per 8-12 hens produces fertile eggs; hens go broody (sit on eggs) naturally or use an incubator (21-day incubation). Goats: breed does in fall (September-November) for spring kidding; gestation is 150 days; a doe typically produces 1-3 kids. Rabbits: does can be bred at 6 months; gestation is 31 days; a doe can produce 6-8 litters per year with 6-8 kits per litter. Keep detailed breeding records including dates, parentage, and outcomes to improve your herd genetics over time.
