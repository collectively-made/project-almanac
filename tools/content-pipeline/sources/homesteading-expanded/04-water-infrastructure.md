# Water Infrastructure for Homesteads

## Well Maintenance

A properly maintained well provides reliable water for decades. Have your well water tested annually for bacteria (total coliform and E. coli), nitrates, pH, and total dissolved solids. Spring testing is especially important as snowmelt and heavy rains can introduce contaminants. Inspect the wellhead annually: the casing should extend at least 12 inches above ground level, the cap should be securely sealed, and there should be no cracks or gaps where surface water could enter. Keep the area around the wellhead clear of chemicals, fertilizers, and animal waste — maintain a minimum 50-foot setback from potential contamination sources.

## Shock Chlorination

If your well tests positive for bacteria, shock chlorinate it. Calculate the volume of water in your well (well depth in feet × gallons per foot based on casing diameter — a 6-inch casing holds 1.5 gallons per foot). Add enough unscented household bleach to achieve 200 ppm chlorine — approximately 3 pints of bleach per 100 gallons of water. Pour the bleach into the well, connect a hose from an outdoor faucet back into the well and recirculate for 30 minutes to mix thoroughly. Open each faucet in the house until you smell chlorine, then close them. Let the solution sit in the well and plumbing for 12-24 hours (do not use the water during this time). Flush the system by running water through an outdoor hose onto a non-vegetated area until the chlorine smell dissipates. Retest in 1-2 weeks.

## Rainwater Collection Systems

A complete rainwater harvesting system includes: gutters and downspouts (clean regularly), a first-flush diverter (discards the first 1-2 gallons of dirty roof runoff per 1,000 sq ft of roof), a leaf screen or filter basket, storage tank (food-grade polyethylene, fiberglass, or concrete), overflow pipe, and a distribution system (gravity-fed or pump). Size your tank based on roof area and local rainfall. A 2,000 sq ft roof in an area with 1 inch of rain collects approximately 1,250 gallons. For drinking water use, filter (minimum 1 micron) and disinfect (UV sterilizer or chlorination) all collected rainwater. Check local regulations — some jurisdictions restrict rainwater harvesting.

## Gravity-Fed Distribution

If your water source (spring, tank, pond) is uphill from your home, gravity provides free water pressure. You need 2.31 feet of elevation for every 1 PSI of pressure — a typical household needs 20-40 PSI, requiring the source to be 46-92 feet above the point of use. Use HDPE (high-density polyethylene) pipe rated for your pressure needs. For every 100 feet of horizontal pipe, add approximately 5% to your pressure calculations to account for friction loss. Use 1-inch or larger pipe for main supply lines to reduce friction. Install a shutoff valve at the source and a pressure-reducing valve at the house if elevation provides excessive pressure. Bury pipe below the frost line to prevent freezing.

## Pond and Cistern Construction

A farm pond provides water for irrigation, livestock, fire suppression, and potentially household use (with treatment). Site ponds in natural drainage areas with clay soil to minimize seepage. A typical half-acre pond 8-10 feet deep holds approximately 1.3 million gallons. Compact the dam in 6-inch lifts with heavy equipment. Install a bottom drain and an overflow spillway sized for a 25-year storm event. For a cistern (underground water tank), reinforced concrete or food-grade plastic tanks buried below the frost line store water reliably. A 1,500-gallon cistern provides a 2-week supply for a family of four at conservative usage (50 gallons per person per day).
