# Medical Emergencies

## Heat Exhaustion and Heat Stroke

Heat exhaustion symptoms: heavy sweating, weakness, cold and clammy skin, fast but weak pulse, nausea, fainting. Treatment: move to a cool area, lie down with feet elevated, loosen clothing, apply cool wet cloths, sip water slowly. Heat stroke is a life-threatening emergency — symptoms: high body temperature (above 103°F/39.4°C), hot and dry skin (no sweating), rapid strong pulse, confusion, unconsciousness. Treatment: call emergency services immediately, move to a cool area, rapidly cool the body using any available method — immersion in cool water, ice packs on neck, armpits, and groin, wet sheets with fanning. Do not give fluids to an unconscious person.

## Hypothermia

Hypothermia occurs when body core temperature drops below 95°F (35°C). Mild symptoms: shivering, poor coordination, slurred speech, poor judgment. Severe symptoms: shivering stops, confusion, drowsiness, slow breathing and pulse. Treatment: get the person out of the cold and wind, remove wet clothing, warm gradually — do not rub extremities. Apply heat to the core (chest, neck, head, groin) using warm blankets, skin-to-skin contact, or warm water bottles wrapped in cloth. Give warm (not hot) sweet drinks if the person is conscious and alert. Do not give alcohol. Severe hypothermia requires professional medical care — handle the person gently, as rough handling can trigger cardiac arrest in severely hypothermic patients.

## Dehydration

Symptoms progress from mild to severe: thirst, dry mouth, dark yellow urine, headache, dizziness (mild); rapid heartbeat, rapid breathing, sunken eyes, lack of sweat, confusion, fainting (severe); very dark or no urine, extreme irritability, unconsciousness (life-threatening). Treatment: replace fluids gradually — sip water or an oral rehydration solution (1 liter water + 6 teaspoons sugar + 1/2 teaspoon salt). Do not drink large amounts rapidly. In a survival situation, prevent dehydration by rationing activity rather than water — rest during the heat of the day, work in the cooler morning and evening hours. Signs of adequate hydration: clear to light yellow urine produced regularly.

## Choking

For a conscious adult or child over 1 year: perform abdominal thrusts (Heimlich maneuver). Stand behind the person, wrap your arms around their waist, place the thumb side of your fist just above the navel, grasp your fist with your other hand, and deliver quick upward thrusts until the object is expelled or the person becomes unconscious. For an unconscious person: lower them to the ground, call for help, begin CPR — each time you open the airway to give breaths, look in the mouth for a visible object and remove it if seen. Do not perform blind finger sweeps. For infants under 1 year: alternate 5 back blows (between the shoulder blades with the heel of your hand) with 5 chest thrusts (two fingers on the breastbone just below the nipple line).

## Allergic Reactions and Anaphylaxis

Mild allergic reactions (hives, itching, localized swelling) can be treated with antihistamines (diphenhydramine/Benadryl). Anaphylaxis is a life-threatening allergic reaction with symptoms: difficulty breathing, swelling of face/throat, rapid pulse, dizziness, nausea, loss of consciousness. Treatment: administer epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen) into the outer thigh if available — this is the only effective treatment and must be given quickly. Call emergency services. Have the person lie flat with legs elevated unless they are having difficulty breathing, in which case let them sit up. A second dose of epinephrine may be needed if symptoms return. Even after epinephrine improves symptoms, the person needs emergency medical care — anaphylaxis can recur hours later (biphasic reaction).

## CPR Basics

For unresponsive adults not breathing normally: call emergency services, begin chest compressions immediately. Place the heel of your hand on the center of the chest (lower half of the breastbone), place your other hand on top, interlock fingers, and push hard and fast — at least 2 inches deep, at a rate of 100-120 compressions per minute (to the beat of "Stayin' Alive"). After 30 compressions, tilt the head back, lift the chin, and give 2 rescue breaths (1 second each, watching for chest rise). Continue 30:2 cycles. If you are untrained or uncomfortable with rescue breaths, compression-only CPR is still effective — push hard and fast without stopping. Use an AED (automated external defibrillator) as soon as one is available — turn it on and follow the voice prompts.
