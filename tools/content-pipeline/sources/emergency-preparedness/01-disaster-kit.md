# Emergency Preparedness Kits

## 72-Hour Emergency Kit

Every household should maintain a kit that sustains each family member for at least 72 hours. Store the kit in an accessible location and check it every six months. Essential contents per person: 1 gallon of water per day (3 gallons total), non-perishable food (canned goods, energy bars, dried fruit, peanut butter), manual can opener, battery-powered or hand-crank radio, flashlight with extra batteries, first aid kit, whistle for signaling, dust masks, plastic sheeting and duct tape for shelter-in-place, moist towelettes, garbage bags and ties, wrench or pliers for turning off utilities, local maps, and cell phone charger (battery bank or solar).

## Important Documents

Store copies of critical documents in a waterproof bag within your emergency kit: identification (driver's license, passport), insurance policies, bank account records, medication list and prescriptions, family contact information, proof of address, and birth certificates. Take photos of these documents on your phone as a backup. Consider storing digital copies in an encrypted cloud service or on a USB drive in a separate location.

## Water Storage for Extended Emergencies

For home water storage beyond 72 hours, store 1 gallon per person per day for a minimum of 2 weeks (14 gallons per person). Use food-grade water storage containers — never containers that previously held chemicals. Commercial water jugs, cleaned 2-liter soda bottles, and dedicated water storage barrels (15-55 gallons) all work. Add 1/8 teaspoon of unscented household bleach per gallon if storing tap water for long periods. Replace stored water every 6-12 months. A water heater tank (typically 40-80 gallons) is an emergency water source — turn off the intake valve and power before draining.

## Power Outage Preparedness

Keep flashlights and batteries in every room. Battery-powered lanterns are safer than candles indoors. A hand-crank or solar-powered radio provides emergency broadcasts when power is out. If using a generator, run it outdoors only — never in a garage, basement, or enclosed area due to carbon monoxide risk. Place carbon monoxide detectors on every level of your home. In winter, close off unused rooms to conserve heat. Open faucets to a slow drip to prevent pipe freezing. Your refrigerator keeps food safe for 4 hours without power if the door stays closed; a full freezer maintains temperature for 48 hours (24 if half full).

## Evacuation Planning

Establish two evacuation routes from your home and neighborhood. Identify a local meeting point (a neighbor's house or nearby landmark) and an out-of-area contact person everyone can call. Keep your vehicle fuel tank at least half full at all times. Pack a go-bag with essentials: the items from your 72-hour kit plus sturdy shoes, weather-appropriate clothing, cash in small bills, medications, and copies of important documents. Know your community's evacuation routes and shelter locations. Practice the plan with your family at least twice a year.

## Sheltering in Place

In some emergencies (chemical spills, radiation events, severe storms), sheltering in place is safer than evacuating. Choose an interior room with no windows, or as few as possible, on the highest floor (for chemical threats — chemicals are often heavier than air). Seal doors, windows, and vents with plastic sheeting and duct tape. Turn off HVAC systems to prevent drawing contaminated air inside. Listen to emergency broadcasts for instructions on when it is safe to ventilate. Keep at least 2 days of water and food in your shelter-in-place location.
