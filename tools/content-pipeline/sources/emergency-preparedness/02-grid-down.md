# Grid-Down Scenarios

## Extended Power Loss

A prolonged power outage (days to weeks) fundamentally changes daily life. Priorities shift to water, food preservation, heating/cooling, communication, and security. Immediately fill bathtubs and large containers with water while municipal pressure holds. Use perishable food first — eat refrigerated items within 4 hours, then move to frozen food, then shelf-stable supplies. Cook perishable food before it spoils. In winter, move the family into one room and use body heat, blankets, and safe heat sources (wood stove, kerosene heater with ventilation).

## Communication Without Infrastructure

When cell towers and internet fail, alternative communication options include: battery-powered AM/FM radio for emergency broadcasts, NOAA weather radio (7 channels with continuous updates), FRS/GMRS two-way radios (range 1-5 miles typical), ham radio (requires license but provides long-range communication), and physical message boards at community gathering points. Keep a hand-crank or solar radio — batteries will eventually run out. Program emergency frequencies into radios in advance. Establish a communication plan with neighbors: specific check-in times, signal flags, or designated meeting spots.

## Food Preservation Without Refrigeration

When the grid goes down, preserve food using non-electric methods. Smoking meats over a low fire (130-170°F) both cooks and preserves — cold-smoked meats last weeks in cool weather. Salt-curing uses 8 pounds of salt per 100 pounds of meat and preserves for months. Root cellaring stores vegetables at 32-40°F using the earth's natural temperature — dig below the frost line. Dehydrating food in the sun or over a fire removes moisture — slice thin (1/4 inch) for even drying. Lacto-fermentation preserves vegetables for months using only salt and water. Rendering fat (lard, tallow) preserves it for months at room temperature.

## Sanitation Without Running Water

Human waste management becomes critical when plumbing fails. A 5-gallon bucket with a seat and heavy-duty garbage bags serves as an emergency toilet. After each use, add a scoop of sawdust, wood ash, or cat litter to reduce odor and absorb moisture. Seal full bags tightly and store away from living areas and water sources. Dig a latrine trench at least 200 feet from water sources, 6-8 inches deep — deeper in cold weather to prevent freezing the surface. Wash hands with water and soap after every use — hand hygiene prevents the waterborne diseases that become epidemic during infrastructure failures.

## Community Organization

Isolated individuals are vulnerable. During extended emergencies, organize with neighbors: pool resources, share skills, coordinate security, and distribute tasks. Identify who has medical training, mechanical skills, gardening knowledge, and ham radio equipment. Establish a watch schedule. Share surplus food and water with those in need — community cohesion is a survival resource. Historical disasters consistently show that organized communities recover faster and with fewer casualties than disorganized ones.
