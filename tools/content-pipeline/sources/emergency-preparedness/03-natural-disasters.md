# Natural Disaster Response

## Earthquake Response

During shaking: Drop, Cover, Hold On. Drop to hands and knees, get under a sturdy desk or table and hold on, or cover your head and neck with your arms if no shelter is available. Stay away from windows, exterior walls, and heavy objects that could fall. If in bed, stay there and protect your head with a pillow. Do not run outside during shaking — most injuries occur from falling debris near exits. If outdoors, move to an open area away from buildings, trees, and power lines. After shaking stops: check for injuries, check for gas leaks (smell, but do not use flames — use a flashlight), and be prepared for aftershocks. Do not re-enter damaged buildings.

## Flood Safety

Never drive through floodwater — 6 inches of moving water can knock you down, 12 inches can carry away a small car, and 2 feet will float most vehicles. If flooding is predicted: move valuables to upper floors, fill bathtubs and containers with clean water, and be ready to evacuate. If ordered to evacuate, do so immediately — take your go-bag and follow designated evacuation routes. If trapped by rising water: go to the highest point available, signal for help, and call emergency services. After floodwater recedes: do not enter flooded buildings until authorities confirm safety. Floodwater is contaminated — assume anything it touched is contaminated. Discard food that contacted floodwater. Disinfect all surfaces with a bleach solution (1 cup per 5 gallons of water).

## Wildfire Preparedness

Create defensible space around your home: Zone 1 (0-5 feet from structure) — use noncombustible materials, remove dead vegetation, keep gutters clean. Zone 2 (5-30 feet) — space trees 10+ feet apart, remove ladder fuels (vegetation that allows fire to climb from ground to treetops), keep grass mowed to 4 inches. Zone 3 (30-100 feet) — thin trees to 10-foot spacing, remove dead wood and debris. If a wildfire approaches: close all windows, doors, and vents. Move flammable furniture away from windows. Leave lights on (visibility for firefighters in smoke). Connect garden hoses. Fill pools, hot tubs, and garbage cans with water. If ordered to evacuate, leave immediately — wildfires can outrun a person and move faster than you expect.

## Winter Storm Survival

Before winter: insulate pipes, service your heating system, stock 3+ days of food and water, ensure you have alternative heat (wood stove, kerosene heater — with ventilation). During a winter storm: stay indoors. If you must go outside, wear multiple layers (wicking base, insulating middle, waterproof outer), cover extremities, and limit exposure time. Frostbite warning signs: numbness, white or grayish-yellow skin, hard or waxy feeling. Treatment: get indoors, warm the affected area gradually in warm (not hot) water (100-105°F), do not rub frostbitten skin. If pipes freeze: open the affected faucet, apply gentle heat (hair dryer, heat lamp, warm towels) starting from the faucet end and working toward the frozen section. Never use an open flame on pipes.

## Tornado Safety

Know the difference: a tornado watch means conditions are favorable for tornado formation (be prepared); a tornado warning means a tornado has been sighted or detected on radar (take shelter immediately). Shelter priorities: underground (basement, storm cellar) is best. If no underground option, go to the lowest floor interior room (closet, bathroom, hallway) away from windows. Get under a sturdy piece of furniture and cover your head. In a mobile home, leave and go to a sturdy building — mobile homes are extremely dangerous in tornadoes regardless of tie-downs. If caught outside with no shelter, lie flat in a low ditch and cover your head. Do not shelter under highway overpasses — wind accelerates through these structures.
