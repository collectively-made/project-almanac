# Wild Food Procurement

## Universal Edibility Test

CAUTION: Never eat any wild plant you cannot positively identify. If identification is uncertain, use the Universal Edibility Test from FM 3-05.70. Test only one plant part at a time. Fast for 8 hours before testing. Place a small piece on your inner wrist for 8 hours — if no reaction, place it on your outer lip for 15 minutes. If no burning or irritation, place it on your tongue for 15 minutes. If no reaction, chew and hold in your mouth for 15 minutes without swallowing. If no ill effects, swallow. Wait 8 hours. If no nausea, vomiting, or other ill effects, eat a small handful. Wait another 8 hours. If still no ill effects, the plant part is likely safe in moderate quantities. This process takes at least 24 hours per plant part.

## Fishing Without Equipment

Improvised fishhooks can be made from thorns, bone, safety pins, or carved hardwood. A gorge hook is a small, sharpened stick tied in the middle to the line — when swallowed by the fish, it turns sideways and catches. A simple fish trap can be built from stones in a stream: construct a V-shaped funnel with the narrow opening pointing downstream and a holding pen beyond. Fish swim in but cannot easily find the narrow exit. Hand fishing (noodling) is possible in warm, slow-moving water by reaching into undercuts and submerged holes where catfish shelter.

## Insect Gathering

Insects are an excellent emergency protein source — pound for pound, many contain more protein than beef. Grasshoppers, crickets, and grubs are widely available and safe to eat when cooked. Remove legs and wings from grasshoppers before eating (the barbed legs can catch in your throat). Cook all insects by roasting near a fire to kill parasites. Avoid brightly colored insects (warning coloration indicates toxicity), hairy caterpillars, insects with a strong smell, and any insect found on or near carrion.

## Trapping Principles

Trapping is more energy-efficient than active hunting. Set multiple traps — even experienced trappers have a low success rate per individual trap. Place traps along animal trails, which are visible as worn paths through vegetation, compressed grass, or tracks in mud. The figure-4 deadfall is one of the simplest effective traps: three sticks notched to form a trigger mechanism that releases a heavy flat rock or log onto the animal when the bait stick is disturbed. Use local materials so the trap blends with the surroundings — animals avoid objects that look out of place.

## Calories and Decision Making

In a survival situation, you burn 2,000-4,000 calories per day depending on activity level, weather, and stress. Procuring food costs energy. Focus food-gathering efforts on the highest calorie-to-effort ratio: fishing in productive waters, gathering nuts and berries in season, setting passive traps. Active hunting burns far more calories than it typically returns unless you are skilled. In short-term survival situations (under a week), prioritize water, shelter, and signaling over food — your body has sufficient energy reserves, but dehydration and exposure kill quickly.
