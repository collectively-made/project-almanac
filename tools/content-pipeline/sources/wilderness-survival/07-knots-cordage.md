# Knots and Cordage

## Essential Survival Knots

Learn these five knots and you can handle most survival situations. The bowline creates a fixed loop that does not slip under load — use it for rescue harnesses, securing shelter ridgelines, and tying around objects. Make a small loop in the line, pass the working end up through the loop, around the standing end, and back down through the loop. The taut-line hitch is adjustable under tension — ideal for guy lines on tarps and tents. It grips when loaded but slides when unloaded to adjust tension. The clove hitch quickly fastens a line to a pole or stake — two loops dropped over a post, or wrapped around and tucked under. It can slip under variable loads, so back it up with a half hitch for security.

## Square Knot and Sheet Bend

The square knot (reef knot) joins two ropes of equal diameter. Right over left, then left over right — if done incorrectly (right over right), you get a granny knot that slips. Never use a square knot for critical loads — it can capsize and fail. For joining ropes of different diameters, use a sheet bend instead. Form a bight in the thicker rope, pass the thinner rope up through the bight, around both legs of the bight, and back under itself. A double sheet bend (wrap twice) is more secure and should be used when the ropes differ significantly in size or when the rope is stiff or slippery.

## Natural Cordage

When commercial rope is unavailable, make cordage from plant fibers. The inner bark of basswood, elm, cedar, and willow can be peeled in strips, soaked, and twisted into serviceable rope. Yucca leaves, cattail leaves, and dogbane stalks provide strong fibers. The reverse-wrap technique produces the strongest cordage: take a bundle of fibers and twist it clockwise while simultaneously wrapping two twisted bundles counterclockwise around each other. This opposing twist locks the fibers together and prevents unraveling. Natural cordage is weaker than commercial rope — use thicker bundles and avoid sudden shock loads.

## Lashing Techniques

Square lashing binds two poles at right angles — essential for building shelters, tripods, and rafts. Start with a clove hitch on one pole, then wrap the rope alternately over and under the poles in a square pattern for 3-4 complete turns. Finish with frapping turns (wrapping tightly between the poles to cinch everything snug) and tie off with a clove hitch. Diagonal lashing joins poles that cross at angles other than 90 degrees — start with a timber hitch, wrap diagonally, then frap. Shear lashing binds two parallel poles, allowing them to be spread apart as an A-frame — wrap around both poles, frap between them, then spread the legs to the desired angle.

## Fishing Line and Snare Wire

For improvised fishing line, braid or twist the finest natural fibers available — milkweed, nettle, or inner bark fibers. The line needs to be thin enough not to alarm fish but strong enough to hold them. Test line strength by hanging a weight before fishing. Snare wire is best made from any available wire — picture hanging wire, electrical wire stripped of insulation, or paracord inner strands. A snare loop should be approximately fist-sized for rabbit-sized game, placed at head height for the target animal on a well-used game trail.
