# Fire Starting and Maintenance

## Fire Lay Preparation

Before attempting to light a fire, prepare your materials in three categories. Tinder must catch a spark and hold a flame — dry grass, shredded bark (birch bark is excellent), dryer lint, cotton balls with petroleum jelly, fine wood shavings, or cattail fluff. Kindling consists of small twigs and sticks from pencil-lead to pencil thickness. Fuel wood ranges from finger-thickness to wrist-thickness pieces. Gather three times more material than you think you will need. All materials should be dead and dry — standing dead wood is drier than wood on the ground.

## Teepee Fire Lay

The teepee fire lay is the most versatile configuration. Place a tinder bundle on the ground. Lean small kindling sticks around the tinder in a cone shape, leaving an opening on the windward side for lighting. As the kindling catches, add progressively larger sticks, maintaining the cone shape. The teepee concentrates heat upward, making it effective for starting fires quickly. Once established, it can be converted to a log cabin or long fire configuration for sustained burning.

## Log Cabin Fire Lay

Stack fuel wood in alternating layers like a log cabin, with the tinder and kindling in the center. This configuration provides a stable, long-burning fire with good airflow. It is excellent for cooking because it creates a bed of coals as the structure collapses. Build it with the largest logs on the bottom and smaller pieces on top.

## Ferro Rod and Striker

A ferrocerium rod (ferro rod) produces sparks at approximately 5,400°F (3,000°C) when scraped with a steel striker. Hold the rod steady and pull the striker toward you (rather than pushing the rod forward, which disturbs the tinder). Direct sparks into the tinder bundle. Once the tinder catches, gently blow at the base to supply oxygen. A ferro rod works when wet — scrape off the oxidized outer layer first. This is one of the most reliable fire-starting tools because it functions in rain, wind, and at any altitude.

## Bow Drill

The bow drill is the most practical friction fire method. You need four components: a fireboard (dry softwood like cottonwood, willow, or cedar), a spindle (same wood, 12-18 inches long, thumb-thick, pointed at both ends), a bearing block (hardwood or stone with a socket), and a bow (a curved stick 2 feet long strung with cordage). Cut a notch in the fireboard to collect the hot dust. Wrap the bow string once around the spindle. Press the spindle into the fireboard socket with the bearing block, and saw the bow back and forth. The friction creates a hot coal in the notch, which you transfer to a tinder bundle and blow into flame. This method requires practice — the wood must be completely dry.

## Fire in Wet Conditions

When everything is wet, look for standing dead wood and split it to access the dry interior. Birch bark contains flammable oils and burns even when wet. Feather sticks — thin curls shaved from a dry wood baton without detaching them — catch fire more easily than solid wood. Build your fire on a platform of green logs to keep it off wet ground. Create a small roof of bark or a tarp over your fire lay while establishing it. Once a fire is burning well, it will dry out and consume damp wood.

## Banking a Fire for the Night

To keep a fire going overnight, let it burn down to a bed of coals. Cover the coals with a thick layer of ash (2-3 inches). Place two or three large, green logs on top. The ash insulates the coals, and the green wood smolders slowly. In the morning, scrape away the ash, expose the coals, and add tinder and kindling to restart. This technique conserves firewood and ensures you have fire in the morning without starting from scratch.
