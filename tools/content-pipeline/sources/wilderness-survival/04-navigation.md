# Land Navigation

## Finding North Without a Compass

The North Star (Polaris) indicates true north in the Northern Hemisphere. Locate the Big Dipper constellation — the two stars forming the outer edge of the cup point toward Polaris, which sits at the tip of the Little Dipper's handle. Polaris is the only star that remains essentially stationary while all other stars rotate around it.

During the day, the shadow-tip method works in any hemisphere. Place a straight stick vertically in the ground. Mark the tip of its shadow with a stone. Wait 15-20 minutes and mark the new shadow tip. A line drawn between the two marks runs approximately east-west, with the first mark being west. A perpendicular line from this gives you north-south.

## Using a Watch as a Compass

In the Northern Hemisphere, point the hour hand at the sun. The midpoint between the hour hand and 12 o'clock points approximately south. In the Southern Hemisphere, point the 12 o'clock mark at the sun — the midpoint between 12 and the hour hand points approximately north. If on daylight saving time, use 1 o'clock instead of 12. This method is approximate and works best in temperate latitudes.

## Natural Navigation Indicators

Several natural features provide directional clues, though none is perfectly reliable alone — use multiple indicators together. In the Northern Hemisphere, moss tends to grow more heavily on the north side of trees (the shaded side), though this is inconsistent in dense forests. Spider webs are often built on the south side of trees where insects are more active in warmth. Snow melts faster on south-facing slopes. Prevailing wind direction can indicate compass direction if you know local weather patterns. Tree growth rings are often wider on the south side where more sunlight is received.

## Terrain Association

Travel along linear features — ridgelines, rivers, trails, power lines, and fence lines — to maintain direction and create a mental map. Before moving, study the terrain from high ground and identify landmarks along your route. Use back-bearings: periodically turn around and memorize what the terrain looks like behind you, so you can recognize it if you need to retrace your route.

## Staying Found

If lost, stop moving immediately. Use the STOP method: Sit down, Think about your situation, Observe your surroundings, Plan your next move. Continuing to walk when disoriented usually makes things worse. If you told someone your plans, stay in one location — search parties will find you faster. If you must move, travel downhill and follow water downstream — streams lead to rivers, rivers lead to settlements. Mark your route as you go with rock cairns, broken branches, or torn cloth so searchers can track you and so you don't travel in circles.
