# Water Procurement and Purification

## Finding Water

Water is your most critical survival need — you can survive weeks without food but only days without water. In most environments, water can be found by following animal tracks downhill, watching the flight patterns of birds (especially at dawn and dusk), looking for green vegetation in arid areas, and checking rock crevices and depressions after rain. In the morning, collect dew from grass and leaves by dragging an absorbent cloth through vegetation and wringing it into a container — you can collect over a quart per hour this way.

## Solar Water Still

In arid environments, construct a solar still by digging a hole 3 feet across and 2 feet deep. Place a collection container in the center. Cover the hole with clear plastic sheeting, sealed around the edges with dirt. Place a small rock in the center of the plastic directly over the container so the plastic forms an inverted cone. Moisture from the ground evaporates, condenses on the plastic, and drips into the container. A solar still can produce 1-3 cups of water per day depending on soil moisture and sunlight. Adding cut vegetation or non-potable water to the hole increases output.

## Boiling

Boiling is the most reliable method to make water safe for drinking. Bring water to a rolling boil for at least 1 minute. At altitudes above 6,500 feet (2,000 meters), boil for 3 minutes because water boils at a lower temperature at altitude. Boiling kills bacteria, viruses, and parasites. If you lack a metal container, you can boil water in a wooden or bark container by placing fire-heated rocks into the water — select dry rocks from high ground to avoid steam explosions from moisture-containing rocks.

## Chemical Purification

Unscented household bleach containing 5-6% sodium hypochlorite can purify water in the field. Add 6 drops (approximately 1/8 teaspoon) per gallon of clear water, or 12 drops per gallon of cloudy water. Stir and let stand for 30 minutes. The water should have a slight chlorine smell after treatment. If it does not, repeat the dose and wait another 15 minutes. Iodine tablets are another effective chemical treatment — follow package directions, typically 1-2 tablets per quart with a 30-minute wait time. Note that iodine is not effective against Cryptosporidium.

## Filtration

A field-expedient filter can remove sediment and some pathogens. Layer materials in a container with holes in the bottom: start with a layer of gravel (2 inches), then coarse sand (2 inches), then fine sand (2 inches), then crushed charcoal from your fire (4 inches), then another layer of fine sand. Pour water through slowly and collect the filtered water below. This filter removes sediment, improves taste, and reduces some bacterial contamination, but it does not guarantee pathogen-free water. Always boil or chemically treat filtered water as well.

## Water Storage

Store purified water in clean, covered containers. If using found containers, sanitize them first with a bleach solution (1 tablespoon per gallon, let sit 30 seconds, rinse). In a survival camp, establish a routine of purifying water in batches and maintaining a reserve. In cold weather, keep water containers close to your body or insulated to prevent freezing. A person needs a minimum of 2 quarts (about 2 liters) of water per day in moderate temperatures, and up to 1 gallon per day in hot conditions or during heavy physical activity.
