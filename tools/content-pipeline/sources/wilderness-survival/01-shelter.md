# Shelter Building

## Shelter Site Selection

Choose a shelter site with care. Look for natural protection from wind — a hillside, dense trees, or rock formation. Avoid hilltops (exposed to wind), valley floors (cold air settles), and dry riverbeds (flash flood risk). Check above for dead branches ("widow makers") that could fall. Ensure the site is near water but not in a flood zone. In cold weather, a south-facing slope receives the most sunlight in the Northern Hemisphere.

## Debris Hut

The debris hut is one of the most effective emergency shelters. It provides excellent insulation and can be built without tools. Start with a ridgepole — a sturdy branch 9-12 feet long — propped against a tree or supported by a tripod at one end. The ridgepole should be just long enough for you to lie beneath. Lean branches against both sides at 45-degree angles, creating a ribbed framework. Starting from the bottom up, pile leaves, pine needles, grass, or other debris at least 2-3 feet thick over the entire structure. The thicker the debris layer, the better the insulation. Stuff the interior floor with dry leaves for ground insulation — cold ground draws heat from your body 25 times faster than cold air.

## Lean-To Shelter

A lean-to is quick to build and provides good protection from wind and rain coming from one direction. Find or construct a horizontal support beam 7-9 feet long between two trees, approximately 3-4 feet off the ground. Lean branches against the beam at 45-60 degrees on the windward side. Layer vegetation, bark, or a tarp over the framework starting from the bottom up, like shingles. Build a reflector fire 3-4 feet in front of the opening — the lean-to reflects heat back toward you. The main disadvantage is exposure from the open side.

## Snow Shelters

In snow conditions, dig a snow cave into a firm snowdrift at least 5 feet deep. Tunnel in and up, creating a sleeping platform higher than the entrance — warm air rises and cold air sinks out the entrance. The walls should be at least 1 foot thick. Poke a ventilation hole through the roof with a stick — carbon dioxide poisoning is a real risk in sealed snow shelters. A snow cave maintains an interior temperature near 32°F (0°C) regardless of outside temperature. A single candle can raise the interior temperature by 10-15°F.

## Tarp Shelters

If you have a tarp or poncho, shelter construction becomes much simpler. The A-frame configuration provides good rain protection: run a ridgeline between two trees at chest height, drape the tarp over it, and stake the edges to the ground. In heavy rain, ensure the tarp angle is steep enough (45+ degrees) for water to run off. Use rocks, logs, or dirt to seal the bottom edges against wind. A tarp can also be configured as a lean-to, open-front shelter with a fire reflector, or a simple ground cloth and overhead cover.

## Insulation Priorities

The ground steals body heat faster than the air. Always prioritize ground insulation over overhead cover. Use whatever is available: leaves (at least 6 inches), pine boughs (lay with stems down), grass, a foam pad, or even a pile of clothing. A bed of dry debris can mean the difference between sleeping and shivering all night. In an emergency, sit on your pack rather than the bare ground.
