# Signaling and Rescue

## Ground-to-Air Signals

Create signals visible from the air. The international distress signal is three of anything — three fires in a triangle, three rock piles, three gunshots. Clear an open area and create a large X or SOS using logs, rocks, or trampled snow. Letters should be at least 10 feet tall to be visible from aircraft. Use color contrast — dark materials on snow, light materials on dark ground. Signal panels or bright clothing spread on the ground are effective. At night, three fires in a triangle are the recognized distress signal.

## Signal Fires

Prepare three signal fires in advance and keep them ready to light quickly. For daytime signaling, add green branches, fresh leaves, or damp grass to a hot fire to create thick white smoke — visible for miles against a dark background. For dark backgrounds (forest, mountains), white smoke stands out best. Against overcast skies, add rubber, oil-soaked cloth, or plastic to create black smoke. At night, the fire itself is the signal — bright, hot fires are visible for miles from the air.

## Signal Mirror

A signal mirror is one of the most effective rescue tools — the flash can be seen for 50+ miles on a clear day. Any reflective surface works: a mirror, polished metal, glass, even a credit card. To aim, hold the mirror near your face and extend your other hand toward the target (aircraft or search party). Adjust the mirror until the reflected light illuminates your extended hand, then slowly move your hand away — the light beam now points toward the target. Sweep the horizon continuously when aircraft are heard, even if not visible.

## Whistle Signals

Three short whistle blasts is the universal distress signal. A whistle carries much farther than the human voice and requires less energy. Commercial signal whistles can be heard up to a mile away. Blow three blasts, pause, repeat. If you hear a response (two blasts means "message received"), continue signaling to guide rescuers to your position.

## Making Yourself Findable

Stay near your last known position or your vehicle/camp — searchers will start looking there. Create a large, visible camp with cleared ground, flagging material, and maintained signal fires. If you must travel, leave clear signs of your direction — rock arrows, broken branches, notes in weatherproof containers. Wear bright colors. At night, use a flashlight or headlamp to signal — even a cell phone screen is visible from a distance. If you hear aircraft, move to the most open ground available and signal immediately.
